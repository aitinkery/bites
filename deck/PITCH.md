## Slide 2 — The problem (the hook)

**8,000 photos. No memory.**

Your camera roll has every meal you've ever eaten worth photographing.

Ask yourself: *what was that incredible ramen place last winter?*

You can't answer.

Yelp tells you what *strangers* think. Google Maps tells you what *advertisers* paid for. The Infatuation tells you what *critics* think.

**Nothing tells you what *you* thought.**

---

## Slide 3 — The thesis (the differentiator)

**Bites is the only food app where the unit is the dish, the audience is yourself, and the function is memory.**

| | Yelp | Beli | The Infatuation | **Bites** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Unit | Restaurant | Restaurant | Restaurant | **Dish** |
| Audience | Aggregate strangers | Your friends | Critics | **You** |
| Function | Discovery | Ranking | Recommendation | **Memory** |
| Privacy | Public profile | Friends-only | N/A | **Yours alone** |

These are not the same product. Yelp can't pivot here without destroying its ad business. Beli can't go dish-level without re-onboarding every user. We are alone in this corner of the market.

---

## Slide 4 — The product (what it does today)

A private, mobile-first, photo-driven food journal.

- **Capture** in <30 seconds: photo, dish, restaurant, rating, notes, tags
- **Recall** in <2 seconds: search by restaurant, dish, city, tag, or freeform
- **Own** your data: works offline, exports to JSON, no account required
- **Share** selectively: copy a saved list, email a curated cookbook

Live now: **https://aitinkery.github.io/bites/**

PWA today. App Store within 30 days of first paying customer.

---

## Slide 5 — Why now (the timing)

Three things make this winnable in 2026 that weren't true in 2020:

1. **AI-native retrieval is finally cheap.** "What do I usually order at Italian places?" was impossible 3 years ago. Now it's a $0.02 query.
2. **Camera-roll fatigue is a documented behavior.** 70% of iPhone users say they "have too many photos to find anything." (Apple's own pitch for Photos AI.)
3. **The vibe-coding stack** has lowered build cost from a $500k MVP to a $50/mo solo founder. We can ship and iterate at the speed of a journalist publishing.

Bites without AI is a tool. Bites with AI is a memory prosthetic. We're building both, in that order.

## Slide 6 — Business model

**Free** unlimited: capture, search, export. Forever.
**Pro** $4.99/mo or $39/yr: cloud sync + AI features + unlimited photo storage.
**Cookbook** $9.99 one-time: a magazine-style PDF of your year of bites.
**Circle** $9.99/mo per host: group dish wishlists + "where should we go" recommender.

Affiliate revenue (Resy, OpenTable, DoorDash) layered on after PMF.

**Permanent rules:**
- ❌ No ads. Ever.
- ❌ No selling user data.
- ❌ No restaurant-paid promotion.

The free tier is genuinely useful. The paid tier earns its keep. People don't pay for ad-free; they pay for capability.

## Slide 8 — Defensibility

Three moats, each compounding:

1. **Personal data moat.** A user with 6 months of bites can't switch — their food memory IS the product. Like Day One for journaling, Strava for running, Notion for second-brain.
2. **Dish-level data structure.** Competitors who index restaurants can't retrofit dish primacy without re-architecting AND re-onboarding. We're 3 years ahead structurally.
3. **AI compounds with personal data.** Yelp's AI can only get smarter on aggregate; Bites' AI gets smarter for *you specifically* the more you use it. This is the durable advantage.

Plus: camera-first capture is lower friction than typing reviews. We win the "added a bite while waiting for the check" use case Yelp never reached.

## What this deck deliberately omits

- Stylized founder origin story — keep it brief; the work is the story
- Five-year hockey stick — implausible at this stage; promises we shouldn't make
- Competition slide that's a logo wall — unnecessary; the table on slide 3 does the work
- Fundraising history / cap table — not material at $0 raised
- "Vision: every meal logged" — too ambitious, sounds like surveillance
- Demo video — live demo beats recorded; if remote, screen-share
