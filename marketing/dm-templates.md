# DM Templates — Personal Outreach

For the 10 people you know personally who eat out a lot. Send these one-on-one, not as a broadcast. Customize each one — the more it sounds like you talking to that specific person, the better.

**Rule of thumb**: each DM should mention something only that person would know. A specific restaurant, a specific meal you had with them, a thing they said about food.

---

## Template 1 — Close foodie friend

Best for: friends you regularly talk about food with. People you've discovered restaurants with.

> Hey — quick favor. I built this thing called Bites: bites.kitchen. It's a private food journal. You save the dish, not the restaurant. No signup, lives in your browser.
>
> Made it because I keep forgetting what I ordered at [SPECIFIC RESTAURANT YOU'VE BEEN TO TOGETHER]. Drives me crazy that there's no good way to remember "the thing I had at X that was so good."
>
> Try it once at your next meal? If it works for you I want to know. If it sucks I want to know harder.

**Why this works**: leads with a specific shared memory, doesn't pretend to be a launch announcement, asks for honest feedback in both directions.

---

## Template 2 — Friend who travels for work or has eaten in lots of cities

Best for: friends with a wider restaurant footprint than you. They have the most painful "what was that place in Tokyo?" problem.

> Hey — built a thing that I think you'd actually use. bites.kitchen
>
> It's a private food journal for the "what was that incredible dish I had in [CITY THEY'VE BEEN TO]" problem. Save the dish, not the place. No signup, no tracking, no algorithm. Lives in your browser.
>
> I know you eat your way through every city you visit. Would love your eyes on it. Even just one bite saved would tell me if the form is too much friction.

**Why this works**: positions Bites as solving their specific pain (the traveling-eater memory problem), the ask is tiny (just one bite).

---

## Template 3 — Friend who's a builder, designer, or PM

Best for: friends who'll appreciate the craft AND give product feedback. They'll critique the form, the tag UI, the analytics setup, all of it.

> Hey — I've been building this in the open. AI as co-founder, dogfooding the product. bites.kitchen
>
> Just hit a milestone where the AI noticed I'd never actually saved a bite myself. We did three together and it found 6 UX bugs in 40 minutes.
>
> Curious if you'd give it 5 minutes — try saving one meal, tell me what feels broken. You're going to see things I won't.
>
> Story version is at bites.kitchen/blog/three-bites.html if you want context first.

**Why this works**: appeals to their craft instinct, frames the ask as expert feedback (flattering), gives them both the product link AND the blog link so they can choose how to engage.

---

## Template 4 — Friend who hates "tech bro" pitches

Some friends will roll their eyes at "an AI is my co-founder." For them, lead with the human side.

> Hey — quick one. Built this app to remember what I order at restaurants, because I have the world's worst food memory. bites.kitchen
>
> No signup, no ads, doesn't ask for permissions. Save the dish, write a note about it, done. That's it.
>
> Wondered if you'd try it once and tell me if it's actually useful or if I built a thing nobody wanted.

**Why this works**: strips out all the AI-startup flavor, leads with the personal pain, ends with a self-deprecating ask.

---

## Template 5 — Someone you've recently eaten with

Best for: friends you've shared a recent specific meal with. The bite is right there waiting to be saved.

> Hey — remember when we got [SPECIFIC DISH] at [SPECIFIC RESTAURANT]? I built a thing for exactly the problem of forgetting which dishes were the good ones.
>
> bites.kitchen — try saving that one as your first bite. It takes about a minute. Curious if the form makes sense as a person who'd actually use it.

**Why this works**: starts with a real shared memory, gives them their first bite for free (no decision fatigue about what to save first), ends with a specific feedback question.

---

## What to do AFTER you send

For each DM you send:

1. **Wait. Don't follow up for at least 48 hours.** Friends will get to it when they get to it.
2. **When they reply**, ask one specific follow-up question. Examples:
   - "Did the form feel too long?"
   - "Did you actually save one or did you bail out?"
   - "What field were you most tempted to leave blank?"
3. **Take notes**. Their answers are the most valuable user research I cannot produce. Drop the findings into `~/projects/bites/USER-RESEARCH.md` or just paste them into our chat — I'll capture them.

## Who NOT to DM

Don't DM:
- Coworkers (work/personal boundary)
- People you don't actually talk to (they'll see right through the cold pitch)
- Bigger names you want to impress (premature; ask them AFTER the product retains people)
- Anyone you'd be embarrassed to admit the product flopped to

## How many to send tonight

10 is the right target. Less than 5 isn't enough signal. More than 15 dilutes your follow-up energy.

**Suggested split:**
- 4 close foodie friends (Template 1 or 5)
- 3 traveling-eater types (Template 2)
- 2 builder/designer/PM friends (Template 3)
- 1 friend who'll keep you honest (Template 4)
